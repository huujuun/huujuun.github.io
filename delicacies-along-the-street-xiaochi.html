Delicacies along the Street - Xiaochi

Sep 29, 2019 • huujuun

Chinese xiaochi can be anything and everything, ranging from simple lamb kebabs or fried pancakes to heaping helpings of noodles, and everything in between. Many of the xiaochi dishes keep the names of their origins, so it is always interesting to discover the story behind the delicacy you’ re eating. The very act of eating these xiaochi is something of a cultural experience.

Tianjin Xiaochi

The traditional and famed xiaochi in Tianjin cannot be missed, should you ever come to visit this charming municipality. The ear-hole fried cake, with a history of more than 80 years, its name from the narrow Far-hole Street in Tianjin’s Beidaguan, where the shop selling it was originally located. Fried Dough Twist in Tianjin are usually stuffed with a variety of fillings. Large dough twists produced by Guifaxiang Shiba Street are the most famous. Chatang is made of baked millet and glutinous millet flour. The way Chatang being served at stalls is as attractive as the soup itself. The water is boiled in a big copper pot whose spout is usually fashioned into a dragon head. While making the soup, the skilled Chatang maker holds several bowls in one hand and pours the boiling water precisely into them from quite a distance.

Guangzhou Xiaochi

Guangzhou Shahe Rice Noodles have originated from Shahe Town, hence the name Shahe Rice Noodles. Shahe Restaurant enjoys the highest reputation of serving the best rice noodles. Another well-known xiaochi in Guangzhou is Tingzai (small boat) Porridge. In the past, men of letters and tourists loved to take a boat tour in the western suburbs of Guangzhou when night fell in summer. Small boats carrying porridge served scholars and tourists taking a boat tour. Later many local restaurants began to prepare such porridge too, and called it “Tingzal Porridge”.

Beijing Xiaochi

It is said that there are over 200 kinds of xiaochi in Beijing, including dishes going with alcohol, such as quick-fried tripe, boiled sheep head. As early as in the Liao and Song dynasties, Beijing douzhi (soybean juice) was very popular, But not all the people can stand the taste of this foul-smelled fermented douzhi.

Lvdagun is a famous Islamic snack in Beijing, made from steamed glutinous millet or sticky rice and filled with red peas. After being cut into blocks, it is rolled in soybean flour. This looks like adonkey rolling on the ground, raising dust, which is why the xiaochi gets the name, Lvdagun. Wandouhuang, prepared with white peas, was a well-known imperial xiaochi in ancient China along with kidney bean cakes and small corn buns.
